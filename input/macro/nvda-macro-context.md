## 3. Semiconductor Supply Chain & Manufacturing Constraints

### TSMC Capacity Constraints (Critical Bottleneck)
- **Blackwell + Rubin Production:** Both rely on TSMC's 4NP process (cutting-edge node)
- **CoWoS-L Packaging Capacity:** NVIDIA securing ~70% of TSMC's CoWoS-L capacity through 2027
- **Current Status:** Fully booked through 2026-2027; capacity bottleneck, not demand constraint
- **TSMC Fab Capacity Expansion:** Arizona Fab 21 now producing at high yield, increasing U.S. capacity
- **Lead Time:** Advanced packaging (CoWoS) has 6-9 month lead times; capacity additions take 18-24 months to ramp

### HBM (High-Bandwidth Memory) Supply
- **HBM3E Demand:** Blackwell requires massive HBM stacks; Rubin uses HBM4 (new generation)
- **Suppliers:** Samsung, SK Hynix, Micron (limited capacity)
- **Supply Trajectory:** HBM revenue forecast to grow from $16B (2024) to >$100B by 2030
- **Constraint:** HBM supply is co-constraint with CoWoS; both critical for NVIDIA's product roadmap

### U.S. Manufacturing Onshoring
- **Biden/Trump Policy (Bipartisan):** Semiconductor manufacturing incentives (CHIPS Act, $52B+ funding)
- **TSMC Arizona:** Ramping capacity on U.S. soil; reduces Taiwan geopolitical risk
- **Impact on NVIDIA:** Positive for supply security; neutral to slightly negative for manufacturing cost (U.S. labor costs higher)

## 4. Export Control & Geopolitical Risks

### China AI Chip Export Restrictions (CRITICAL NEAR-TERM ISSUE)

#### Timeline of Policy Changes
1. **April 2025:** Trump administration restricted AI chip sales to China (review period announced)
2. **June 2025:** NVIDIA CEO stated company would exclude China from forward guidance due to uncertainty
3. **July 2025:** Three months into restrictions, Trump backtracked; H20 licenses began being approved
4. **December 2025:** Trump administration announced conditional approval for H200 exports to China
5. **January 2026:** Department of Commerce published regulation permitting H200 sales to China with conditions
6. **Current Status (March 2026):** H200 exports conditionally approved; H20 restricted; policy unsettled

#### Financial Impact
- **2024 China Revenue:** $17B (13% of total sales)
- **2025 China Impact:** Estimated -$2.5B in missed revenue due to April restrictions
- **Estimated Lost Revenue (Apr-Dec 2025):** $3-5B (annualized from 13% baseline)
- **2026 Outlook:** If H200 exports remain permitted, China could return to $15-18B annual revenue; if restrictions tightened, could drop to $5B

#### Policy Risk Factors
- **Consensus Uncertainty:** Proposed rules not yet finalized; may change before implementation
- **Congressional Opposition:** Debate continues on whether exports strengthen or weaken U.S. AI competitiveness
- **Trump Administration Volatility:** Policy changed 4 times in 9 months (Apr → Jun → Jul → Dec → Jan); high uncertainty
- **Probability Scenarios:**
  - **Bull Case (40%):** H200 exports sustained through 2026-2027; China revenue returns to $15B+ annually
  - **Base Case (35%):** Restricted to lower-capability chips; China revenue stays $8-12B annually
  - **Bear Case (25%):** Further tightening; China revenue drops to $3-5B annually

### Other Geopolitical Risks
- **Taiwan Strait Tension:** NVIDIA depends on TSMC (Taiwan-based). War scenario would be catastrophic ($0 revenue scenario, but <1% probability)
- **Japanese Export Controls:** Japan has implemented semiconductor export controls to Russia; unlikely to expand to China (allies differ on China policy)
- **EU Regulation:** Minimal AI chip export restrictions from EU; EU focused on antitrust/competition issues

## 5. Regulatory & Antitrust Risks

### Antitrust Scrutiny
- **FTC Investigation:** Not currently under active FTC antitrust investigation
- **EU DMA (Digital Markets Act):** Not classified as "gatekeeper" (size + market position could change this)
- **Risk Level:** Low near-term, medium-term if market share remains >80% and EU expands DMA scope
- **Precedent:** Intel faced antitrust penalties in EU 2009; AMD was complainant (now in different position)

### Software & Standards Regulation
- **CUDA Licensing:** No threat to proprietary CUDA licensing model currently
- **Open Standards:** Industry pushback on vendor lock-in minimal (customers value CUDA)
- **Risk Level:** Low; software moat is legally defensible

### Data Privacy & AI Safety Regulation
- **EU AI Act:** Compliance requirements for AI systems but not chip manufacturers directly
- **U.S. AI Regulation:** Executive orders on AI safety but no semiconductor-specific restrictions (yet)
- **Impact on NVIDIA:** Indirect (affects customer demand) but not direct regulatory burden

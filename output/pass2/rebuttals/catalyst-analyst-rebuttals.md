## Response to DCF Analyst's Critiques

### MI450 On-Time Probability of 75% Is Too High (Should Be 40-50%)
**Verdict:** Partially Accept

The DCF Analyst argues that my 75% on-time probability is inconsistent with my own acknowledgment of the SemiAnalysis report and that "the truth lies between Q3 2026 and Q2 2027." This is a fair point -- the language I used does imply more uncertainty than the 75% figure reflects. However, 40-50% is too aggressive a downward revision. The SemiAnalysis report is a single Tier 3 source contradicted by multiple Tier 1-2 data points: AMD management confirmation at Q4 earnings (Feb 3), CES 2026, and Morgan Stanley conference (March 3); Oracle's commitment to Q3 2026 deployment of 50,000 MI450 GPUs; and TSMC's N3 process already being in volume production for other customers. I revise from 75% to 60-65% on-time probability, which splits the difference and appropriately weights management's repeated confirmations against a single industry analyst report. This reduces MI450 catalyst EV from +5.9% to approximately +3.5-4.0%.

### Time-Weighted 12-Month Return of +25-40% Is a Conditional Right Tail, Not Expected Value
**Verdict:** Accept

The DCF Analyst correctly identifies that the +25-40% figure assumes positive catalyst stacking and corresponds to a conditional outcome, not a probability-weighted expected value. This presentation creates anchoring bias. Using my own probability-weighted catalyst EVs (MI450 revised +3.5-4.0%, OpenAI/Meta +1.8%, other catalysts +2-3%), the probability-weighted 12-month catalyst contribution is approximately +8-12%. This is consistent with the DCF Analyst's +17% upside to $223 fair value and the Quant Analyst's +7.7% comps EV. I revise the headline return to +10-15% expected (probability-weighted) with a +25-40% conditional upside if catalysts stack positively. The distinction is material for the Director's final assessment.

### Negative Catalysts Should Be Netted Into Expected Return, Not Siloed in Risk Section
**Verdict:** Accept

The DCF Analyst makes a valid structural critique: positive catalysts were probability-weighted into expected returns while negative catalysts (Vera Rubin, AI capex correction) appeared only as risk factors. A balanced catalyst EV should net both. Incorporating Vera Rubin (-2-4% EV at 40% probability), AI capex correction risk (-4-7% EV at 20% probability), and China export controls (-1.7% EV at 30% probability), the net 12-month expected return adjusts from +10-15% to approximately +5-10%. This is a more honest representation and aligns better with the DCF and comps-implied returns.

## Response to Competitive Analyst's Critiques

### 75% On-Time Probability Conflicts with CoWoS Supply Constraints
**Verdict:** Partially Accept

The Competitive Analyst adds an important dimension: MI450 timing depends not just on AMD's execution but on TSMC's CoWoS allocation, where AMD has only 8-10% versus NVIDIA's 60%. This is a supply-side constraint I underweighted. However, the mega-deals (OpenAI, Meta, Oracle) provide AMD with the contractual basis to demand incremental CoWoS allocation from TSMC's expansion to 130,000 wafers/month by late 2026. AMD's absolute allocation can increase even without gaining share of existing capacity. I accept the on-time probability revision to 60-65% (already adjusted), with the CoWoS constraint as a key reason for not going higher.

### Add 3-6 Month Software Readiness Lag for MI450 Deployment
**Verdict:** Partially Accept

The point about MI450 requiring longer time-to-productive-deployment than Vera Rubin due to ROCm versus CUDA readiness is valid for enterprise customers. However, for the three mega-deal customers (OpenAI, Meta, Oracle), this lag is substantially reduced because they have invested in ROCm adaptation and/or have custom software stacks. Since these three customers represent the majority of MI450's initial volume, the effective software lag for revenue-relevant deployments is 1-2 months, not 3-6 months. I accept a 1-2 month lag for mega-deal customers but note the 3-6 month lag applies to second-tier enterprise adoption, which is a 2027 catalyst, not H2 2026.

### Add Broadcom Custom ASIC Milestones as Separate Negative Catalyst
**Verdict:** Accept

This is a genuine analytical gap. Broadcom's custom ASIC deals (OpenAI 10 GW, Google, others) represent dateable competitive events that should be tracked independently, not buried in a line item. I accept the recommendation to add Broadcom earnings calls (March, June, September, December 2026) as catalyst monitoring dates with a -2-4% expected impact if ASIC production readiness is announced. This reduces the net 12-month catalyst-adjusted return by 1-2 percentage points.

## Response to Macro Analyst's Critiques

### Macro Headwinds Can Overwhelm Individual Catalysts (2022 Analog)
**Verdict:** Partially Accept

The Macro Analyst's core argument -- that AMD fell 65% in 2022 despite strong earnings because macro regime dominated -- is historically accurate. However, AMD's business profile has changed materially since 2022: data center revenue was $6.0B then versus $16.6B now, and AMD has $200B+ in contracted pipeline that did not exist in 2022. The 2022 analog applies to a macro-driven selloff but understates AMD's fundamental floor from contracted revenue. I accept a 30-40% discount on positive catalyst impact from macro headwinds (not 50-70%), which reduces the expected return from +5-10% to approximately +3-7%. The phased entry strategy already addresses this risk by delaying capital deployment until catalysts confirm.

### MI450 Delay Probability Should Be 30-35% Due to TSMC Energy/Macro Risk
**Verdict:** Partially Accept

The macro transmission mechanism (Hormuz crisis affecting TSMC energy costs in Taiwan) is real but second-order. Taiwan imports 97% of its energy, but TSMC has invested heavily in on-site power generation and has strategic energy reserves. The likelihood that energy constraints specifically delay AMD's CoWoS allocation is low relative to more direct risks (packaging yield, HBM4 supply). I accept a modest upward revision of delay probability to 35% (from my revised 35-40% range), which is consistent with the macro risk without overweighting an indirect transmission mechanism.

### Add Symmetric Negative Macro Catalyst Section
**Verdict:** Accept

This is a valid structural critique. The catalyst calendar should include negative macro catalysts (Hormuz escalation, Fed meeting surprises, China trade retaliation, Trump-Xi summit March 31) with the same probability/magnitude/timing framework used for positive catalysts. I accept this recommendation and note it reduces the net expected return by an additional 2-3 percentage points when fully integrated, consistent with my revised +3-7% range.

## Response to Devil's Advocate's Critiques

### MI450 On-Time Probability Should Be 40-50% Based on Semiconductor Base Rate
**Verdict:** Partially Accept

The Devil's Advocate provides the most thorough historical argument: Intel denied Sapphire Rapids delays for 18 months, NVIDIA's Hopper was delayed, AMD's own MI300X was 2 quarters late, and the semiconductor industry base rate for on-time delivery at a new process node is 40-50%. This is a strong argument from precedent. However, MI450 has three de-risking factors that precedent does not fully capture: (1) TSMC N3 is not a new node bring-up -- it is in volume production for Apple; (2) AMD has three pre-committed customers with contracted volumes, creating supply chain certainty; (3) the Helios rack-scale system was publicly demonstrated at CES 2026. I maintain 60-65% on-time probability (revised from 75%), which is above the generic base rate but below management's implicit 90%+ confidence, reflecting these AMD-specific factors.

### "Beat and Fade" Pattern Should Discount All Positive Catalyst Magnitudes by 30-50%
**Verdict:** Partially Accept

The observation that AMD's Q4 beat (+23% EPS, only +1.8% AH) reflects a market regime where beats are expected and only exceptional news moves the stock is well-taken. I accept a 20-25% discount on positive catalyst magnitudes (not 30-50%) because the beat-and-fade pattern reflects earnings expectations specifically, not all catalysts. Product launch confirmations (MI450 shipping) and deployment milestones (Oracle supercluster live) are categorically different from earnings beats -- they resolve binary uncertainty rather than exceeding consensus estimates. The discount applies to earnings catalysts but should be lower (10-15%) for product/deployment catalysts.

### NVIDIA Vera Rubin Should Be Primary Negative Catalyst at 50% Probability, -10-15% Impact
**Verdict:** Partially Accept

I accept that NVIDIA benchmark releases have historically caused 10-15% AMD declines, and the DA's evidence from MI250 vs. A100 and MI300 vs. H100 cycles is relevant. However, the current cycle differs because AMD now has three committed hyperscaler partners who have made infrastructure investments specific to MI450 architecture. Switching costs create a 12-18 month insulation window even if Vera Rubin benchmarks favorably. I revise NVIDIA Vera Rubin to 45% probability (up from 40%) with -7-12% impact (up from -5-10%), producing an EV of approximately -3.4-5.4% (up from -2-4%). This is the most significant negative catalyst adjustment.

## Response to Forensic Analyst's Critiques

### MI450 Probabilities Lack Verifiable Production Commitments
**Verdict:** Partially Accept

The Forensic Analyst correctly notes that AMD has used only qualitative language ("on track") without binding quantitative commitments for MI450. This is a legitimate forensic observation -- "on track" is indeed the weakest form of confirmation. However, the Oracle 50,000 MI450 GPU deployment (Q3 2026, publicly announced with specific dates) and TSMC's capacity expansion plans are verifiable third-party commitments that provide indirect production confirmation. I accept that the on-time probability should carry a [CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM] tag rather than the implicit high confidence, and the 60-65% revised probability already reflects this uncertainty.

### "Beat and Fade" Should Account for $306M Inventory Reserve Release Inflating Q4 Results
**Verdict:** Accept

The Forensic Analyst provides a material insight: the Q4 23% EPS beat was partially driven by a $306M inventory reserve release that inflated gross margins by ~300bps. The "true" beat excluding this one-time item was 15-18%, not 23%. This changes the interpretation of the beat-and-fade from a pure market pattern to a fundamentally justified repricing where the market correctly identified the non-recurring component. I accept this correction and note that the Q1 2026 earnings catalyst should be evaluated against the 55% gross margin guidance (which excludes the reserve release), not against the inflated 57% Q4 print.

### Phased Entry Should Include Forensic Checkpoint on Q1 Gross Margins
**Verdict:** Accept

The recommendation to condition the second tranche on organic gross margin improvement (excluding one-time items) is a practical risk discipline measure. If Q1 gross margins revert to ~54% (below the 55% guide), even on a revenue beat, it signals margin pressure that would reduce forward earnings estimates. I accept this as an additional criterion for the second tranche deployment.

### Add TSMC Capacity Expansion Milestones as Explicit Supply-Side Catalysts

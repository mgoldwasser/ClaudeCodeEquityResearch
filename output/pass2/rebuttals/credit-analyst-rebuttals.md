## Response to DCF Analyst's Critique

### Total Debt Discrepancy ($3,300M vs. $4,010M)
**Verdict:** Partially Accept

The DCF Analyst flags a $710M discrepancy between my $3,300M total debt figure (sourced from AMD 10-K/A, Tier 1) and the $4,010M used in the DCF (sourced from market data, Tier 4). My $3,300M reflects aggregate principal outstanding per the 10-K/A filing, which is the authoritative source. The $4,010M likely includes unamortized premium, accrued interest, or operating lease liabilities depending on the data aggregator's definition. I partially accept: while my number is correct on a principal basis, I should have explicitly reconciled the two figures to eliminate cross-analyst confusion. The equity impact ($0.38/share) is immaterial, but data consistency across the team matters for credibility.

### TSMC Purchase Commitment Cash Flow Impact Under Stress
**Verdict:** Accept

The DCF Analyst correctly notes that I identified the $8.5B FY2026 TSMC take-or-pay risk but did not fully quantify the FCF impact under a demand collapse scenario. I estimated $2-4B in cash consumption, but I should have modeled a specific scenario: if AI GPU demand falls 40%, AMD would absorb approximately $3-4B in excess inventory from honored TSMC commitments, reducing bear case FCF by that amount. I accept this critique and note the DCF Analyst's suggestion to incorporate a one-time $2-4B working capital hit into the bear case FCF is analytically sound and consistent with my own liquidity analysis.

### M&A Capacity as a Value Driver
**Verdict:** Accept

The suggestion to frame the $15-23B M&A capacity as a real option with quantifiable value is constructive. My analysis presented M&A capacity as a static number without assigning probability-weighted value to potential accretive acquisitions. The DCF Analyst's estimate of $3-5/share in expected value from a 5% probability of a $5B accretive acquisition is reasonable. I accept this addition for the Director's synthesis.

## Response to Quant Analyst's Critique

### TTM vs. Forward EBITDA for Leverage Calculation
**Verdict:** Partially Accept

The Quant Analyst argues I should use NTM EBITDA (~$12,500M) rather than TTM GAAP EBITDA (~$7,768M) for capacity assessment, which would show forward Debt/EBITDA of ~0.27x and modestly higher M&A capacity ($25B vs. $23B at 2.0x leverage). I partially accept: forward EBITDA is appropriate for assessing borrowing capacity, and lenders would underwrite to forward earnings. However, credit analysis convention uses trailing EBITDA for leverage ratios because it reflects realized earnings, not projections. I should have presented both: TTM leverage (0.42x, conservative) and NTM leverage (0.27x, capacity-oriented). The $2B difference in M&A capacity is immaterial to the thesis.

### Purchase Commitment/Revenue Ratio Benchmarking
**Verdict:** Accept

The Quant Analyst correctly notes that I flagged the $8.5B FY2026 TSMC commitment as a risk without benchmarking it against industry norms. At 18.5% of NTM revenue, AMD's commitment ratio is comparable to NVIDIA's (~20.8%) and within normal fabless semiconductor operating levels. I accept: adding this peer comparison rebalances the risk framing from "hidden liquidity risk" to "standard operating commitment with tail risk if demand collapses." The risk is real but not differentiated relative to peers.

### Warrant Impact on Enterprise Value
**Verdict:** Accept

The Quant Analyst raises a material point: 320M warrant shares at $0.01 exercise price are economically equivalent to issued shares for EV calculation purposes. Including them increases EV from $307B to ~$369B and changes EV/EBITDA from 24.6x to 29.5x. My analysis treated warrants as equity dilution separate from the capital structure, but I should have explicitly modeled the fully-diluted EV to ensure cross-analyst consistency. I accept this critique and note that a "fully diluted capital structure" table should be added showing the warrant impact on enterprise value and per-share metrics.

### TSMC Commitment as Competitive Inflexibility

The credit analysis correctly identifies the cash flow exposure ($2-4B potential drain); the competitive implications of capacity lock-in are better addressed in the competitive analysis. However, incremental R&D is an operating expense that flows through the income statement, not a balance sheet commitment. It reduces FCF available for M&A, not the debt capacity calculation. My M&A capacity figures ($15-23B) reflect balance sheet capacity at target leverage ratios, which is correct. The practical capacity is lower because FCF must also fund operations, but this is a capital allocation question, not a credit metric. I partially accept and note that "deployable M&A capacity" (after ongoing R&D and operating needs) is lower than "theoretical M&A capacity."

## Response to Macro Analyst's Critique

### "Fortress Balance Sheet" Overstatement Given TSMC Commitments
**Verdict:** Partially Accept

The Macro Analyst argues that under a severe scenario (revenue -25%, TSMC commitments fixed), net cash could fall from $7.25B to $3-4B within 18 months. The math is reasonable: $8.5B in commitments against declining FCF would consume a meaningful portion of the cash buffer. I partially accept the numerical point but reject the characterization that "fortress" is overstated. Even at $3-4B net cash, AMD would maintain investment-grade credit quality, full access to capital markets, and no covenant issues. The balance sheet is a fortress — the garrison may be smaller after absorbing a hit, but the walls hold. I revise to: "unambiguous source of strength with $3-5B cushion erosion possible under severe stress."

### M&A Capacity at Current Market Rates
**Verdict:** Accept

The Macro Analyst correctly notes that my M&A capacity figure assumes AMD can access debt at historical rates, while current IG corporate bond yields for A-rated issuers are approximately 5.5-6.5% for 10-year maturities — significantly above AMD's 3.6% weighted average. I accept: the M&A capacity is technically correct in terms of balance sheet capacity, but the cost of executing a large deal is materially higher in the current rate environment. A $15B debt-funded deal at 6.0% would add ~$900M in annual interest expense versus ~$540M at 3.6%, reducing FCF by $360M. I should have stress-tested M&A capacity at current market rates.

### Time-Series Balance Sheet Stress Test
**Verdict:** Accept

The suggestion to model net cash trajectory over 6 quarters under a revenue -25% scenario is constructive. My analysis is point-in-time; a trajectory analysis showing quarterly cash depletion would be more useful for position sizing and risk management. I accept this recommendation and note that even under the stressed trajectory, AMD would not approach covenant breach or rating downgrade territory within the modeled period.

## Response to Risk Analyst's Critique

### TSMC Take-or-Pay Exposure Higher Than Estimated
**Verdict:** Partially Accept

The Risk Analyst argues that the $2-4B cash consumption estimate is too low in a true AI winter, suggesting the full $8.5B obligation could come due while FCF simultaneously declines. I partially accept the directional argument but note that "AI winter" is an extreme tail scenario. Even in the crypto winter of 2022-2023, AMD's revenue declined ~25%, not 60%. The $2-4B estimate assumes partial mitigation (renegotiation with TSMC, redirection of some capacity to non-AI products, inventory buildup rather than write-off). A full $8.5B cash drain with zero mitigation would require a complete and simultaneous collapse of all AI demand — possible but improbable. I revise the range to "$2-6B depending on severity," but maintain that $6-8B would require an unprecedented and sustained demand collapse.

### Intersection of TSMC Commitments + Warrant Dilution
**Verdict:** Accept

The Risk Analyst identifies a genuine blind spot: in a scenario where AI demand softens but deployment milestones are still partially met, AMD faces both cash drain from TSMC commitments and dilution from partially vesting warrants. The compound effect on per-share FCF could be 25-35% — worse than either risk in isolation. This intersection falls between credit and equity analysis and I should have modeled it. I accept and recommend the Director include a combined stress scenario in the final note.

### M&A Event Risk in the Risk Framework
**Verdict:** Partially Accept

The Risk Analyst assigns 15-20% probability to a large M&A event that would shift leverage from 0.42x to 2-3x and potentially trigger a rating downgrade. While Lisa Su has been acquisitive (Xilinx $49B, ZT Systems $4.9B), a $20B+ debt-funded deal in the current environment is unlikely given: (1) AMD is focused on organic MI450 execution, (2) the warrant-driven mega-deals consume management bandwidth, and (3) rating agencies would likely place the A/A1 rating on negative watch. I assign lower probability (5-10%) to a leverage-changing acquisition within 12 months but accept that it should be flagged as a contingent credit risk.

## Response to Devil's Advocate's Critique

### Balance Sheet Strength Enabling Aggressive Commitments
**Verdict:** Partially Accept

The Devil's Advocate argues that credit strength creates a false sense of security — the fortress balance sheet enables the $12.2B TSMC commitments that become the execution problem if demand disappoints. The $800M China-related inventory charge is cited as precedent; a $2-4B TSMC charge would be 3-5x larger and signal mega-deal thesis failure. I partially accept: there is a valid causal link between balance sheet strength and management's willingness to make aggressive commitments. However, I reject the characterization that this makes the credit analysis "incomplete" — my report explicitly identified the $8.5B TSMC commitment as "the single biggest credit risk." The operational signal of a write-down (mega-deal thesis in doubt) is properly the domain of the Competitive and Catalyst Analysts, not the credit analysis. The credit analysis correctly assesses that the balance sheet can absorb the financial hit.

### Warrants as Quasi-Debt Obligation
**Verdict:** Reject

The Devil's Advocate suggests treating 320M warrant shares at $0.01 as economically equivalent to $61B in zero-coupon convertible bonds. This analogy is analytically misleading. Convertible bonds create cash obligations (interest, principal repayment); warrants at $0.01 do not. Warrants dilute existing equity holders but create no claims on the company's assets or cash flows. Including warrants in a "total economic claims / EBITDA" metric conflates equity dilution with credit obligations. The standard credit framework correctly treats warrants as equity instruments. That said, the Quant Analyst's point about including warrants in fully-diluted EV calculations is valid for equity valuation purposes — but this is not a credit metric.

### Elevating TSMC Commitment to Primary Risk
**Verdict:** Partially Accept

The Devil's Advocate wants the TSMC commitment elevated from a secondary finding to a primary risk. My report already identifies it as "the single biggest credit risk" in the Credit Summary section — it is the primary risk. I partially accept that the earlier sections may underweight it relative to the summary, and the framing should be consistent throughout: the TSMC take-or-pay exposure is the only scenario that meaningfully draws down the cash buffer, even though it does not threaten solvency.

## Response to Forensic Analyst's Critique

### GAAP EBITDA Estimation Methodology
**Verdict:** Partially Accept

The Forensic Analyst correctly notes that my GAAP EBITDA of ~$7,768M is estimated rather than calculated directly from the 10-K cash flow statement's D&A line. The ~$2.25B Xilinx intangible amortization drives a meaningful portion of the add-back. If GAAP EBITDA is $6.5B-$7.8B depending on methodology, Debt/EBITDA ranges from 0.42x to 0.51x. I partially accept: both figures still indicate fortress-level creditworthiness, and the conclusion is robust across this range. However, I should have sourced D&A directly from the cash flow statement and presented the EBITDA calculation transparently. The imprecision is a process failure, not an analytical failure.

### Purchase Commitment Granularity (Take-or-Pay vs. Flexible)
**Verdict:** Accept

The Forensic Analyst raises an important point: the $12.2B in purchase commitments may include both unconditional take-or-pay obligations and flexible volume-dependent agreements. I treated the entire $8.5B FY2026 amount as take-or-pay risk, but if only 50% is truly unconditional, the downside liquidity exposure is $4.25B, not $8.5B. This is a meaningful distinction that I should have investigated by reading the purchase commitment footnote in the 10-K. I accept this critique and flag the commitment breakdown as a priority data gap.

### Warrant Accounting Treatment (ASC 815)
**Verdict:** Accept

The Forensic Analyst raises a sophisticated concern: AMD's warrants at $0.01 with performance vesting may not qualify for equity classification under ASC 815-40, potentially requiring liability treatment with quarterly fair value adjustments. If classified as liabilities, warrant mark-to-market charges could create significant GAAP earnings volatility and potentially affect covenant calculations that reference net income or EBITDA. I accept this is a legitimate accounting risk I should have flagged. While the credit rating is not at risk, the potential for large non-cash P&L swings from warrant mark-to-market is a material disclosure item.

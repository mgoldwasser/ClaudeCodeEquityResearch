## Executive Credit Summary

META's balance sheet is, on conventional metrics, investment-grade in the top decile of corporate borrowers: Moody's Aa3 (stable), gross leverage of 0.86x EBITDA, interest coverage of ~26x, and $81.6B in liquid assets. The credit profile is **not currently in distress** and is not near distress under base case assumptions.

The credit concern worth quantifying is not default risk — it is **financial flexibility erosion**. Management has elected to spend $125B (midpoint) on AI infrastructure in 2026 while simultaneously maintaining a $50B buyback authorization, $5B+ in annual dividends, and an unquantified but growing legal liability book. This program will compress free cash flow from $43.6B in FY2025 to an estimated $9-19B in FY2026 and potentially zero at the high end of CapEx guidance. The cash pile will absorb the deficit, but the equity story shifts from "cash return machine" to "capital-intensive infrastructure bet" in a single year.

**Bottom line:** The balance sheet can support the AI CapEx program without threatening the Aa3 rating through 2027. The risk is not solvency — it is opportunity cost, capital misallocation, and what happens in year three if AI infrastructure ROI fails to materialize.

## 3. Debt Sustainability Under CapEx Ramp

### 3.1 FY2026 FCF Sustainability Model

**Central Question:** Can META fund $125B (midpoint) CapEx + $26B capital returns from operating cash flow without materially drawing on its cash buffer or issuing net new debt beyond current authorization?

**FY2026 Assumptions:**
- Revenue: $255.2B (consensus, +27% YoY) [ASSUMPTION]
- OCF Margin: ~54% (compressed from 57.6% in FY2025 as tax normalizes to 13-16% but D&A increase boosts OCF vs. net income) [ASSUMPTION]
- OCF 2026E: **~$137.8B**
- Finance lease payments: ~$3.5B (up from $2.5B in FY2025) [ASSUMPTION]
- CapEx range: $115B–$135B (per guidance)
- Dividends: ~$5.1B ($2.00/share × ~2,550M shares)
- Buybacks: variable — key swing factor

| CapEx Scenario | OCF | CapEx + Leases | FCF | Dividends | Buybacks (est.) | Net Cash Need |
|---------------|-----|----------------|-----|-----------|----------------|--------------|
| Low: $115B | $137.8B | $118.5B | $19.3B | $5.1B | ~$20B | **−$5.8B** |
| Mid: $125B | $137.8B | $128.5B | $9.3B | $5.1B | ~$10B | **−$5.8B** |
| High: $135B | $137.8B | $138.5B | −$0.7B | $5.1B | $0B | **−$5.8B** |

**Conclusion:** Under the base/midpoint scenario ($125B CapEx), META will generate approximately $9.3B in reported FCF, which after dividends ($5.1B) leaves only ~$4.2B for buybacks — a massive reduction from $26.2B in FY2025. The $50B buyback authorization will remain substantially unexecuted. The net cash deficit of approximately $5-18B (depending on buyback pace) will be funded by drawing on the $81.6B cash balance or modest incremental debt issuance. Neither threatens the credit.

**Year-end 2026 liquidity estimate:** Starting from $81.6B, absorbing ~$6-18B of deficit across scenarios, year-end cash would remain in the $63-76B range — still substantial and well above any near-term debt maturity concern.

### 3.2 Incremental Debt Assessment

META need not issue significant incremental debt in 2026 given its liquidity position. The base case is cash draw-down, not debt issuance. If management chooses to issue additional debt to preserve liquidity optionality (as is common for Aa-rated companies), it would likely be in the $15-25B range — keeping total debt at $99-109B. At consensus EBITDA of ~$118B (FY2026E using ~$255B revenue × 46% operating margin + D&A), this keeps gross leverage comfortably below 1.0x.

## 4. Rating Sustainability Analysis

### 4.1 Moody's Aa3 — Current Status

Moody's affirmed Aa3 (stable) in connection with META's FY2025 borrowings and explicitly projected debt-to-EBITDA of <1x for both 2026 and 2027. This affirmation incorporates knowledge of the CapEx ramp — Moody's is not surprised by the $115-135B guidance. The rating reflects:
- Exceptional market position (3.58B daily active people)
- Strong, diversified cash flow generation
- Management's track record of cost discipline (2022-2023 efficiency gains)
- Conservative historical leverage

### 4.2 At What CapEx Level Does Aa3 Come Under Pressure?

Holding all other variables constant (FY2025 OCF of $115.8B, total debt flat at $83.9B), the FCF/Total Debt ratio — a key Moody's metric — falls to critical levels as follows:

| CapEx Level | FCF | FCF/Total Debt | Rating Signal |
|-------------|-----|----------------|---------------|
| $100B | $15.8B | 18.8% | Warning zone |
| $103B | $12.6B | 15.0% | Pressure threshold |
| $115B (guidance low) | $0.8B | 1.0% | Severe pressure — but offset by strong EBITDA |
| $125B (guidance mid) | −$9.7B | Negative | Not applicable — Moody's uses EBITDA-based metrics |

**Important caveat:** Moody's does not use FCF/Debt in isolation. For a company with $98B+ EBITDA, the EBITDA-based leverage metrics (Debt/EBITDA, EBITDA/Interest) dominate the rating. Even at $125B CapEx, if projected EBITDA rises to ~$118B in FY2026, gross leverage remains below 1.0x (even with some debt growth). The Aa3 is **not at material risk under the base or high CapEx scenarios in 2026-2027.**

The rating would come under pressure in a scenario where CapEx is permanently elevated at $125B+ AND revenue growth disappoints materially (e.g., 10-15% vs. 27% consensus) for two or more consecutive years. That scenario would require a simultaneous advertising downturn and AI monetization failure — a tail risk, not a base case.

## 10. Credit Conclusion

### Is META's Balance Sheet Strong Enough to Support the AI CapEx Thesis?

**Yes, but with meaningful trade-offs.**

The balance sheet is objectively strong. Aa3, 0.86x gross leverage, 26x interest coverage, and $81.6B in liquid assets place META in the top tier of global corporate credit. The company can fund $125B in CapEx in 2026 without touching its rating or creating debt service risk. Moody's has already confirmed this with its stable outlook.

The credit analysis does not support a bearish conclusion on near-term solvency or financial distress. The relevant questions for equity investors are more nuanced:

### Financial Flexibility — What Is Being Given Up?

| Metric | FY2024 | FY2025 | FY2026E (Base) |
|--------|--------|--------|----------------|
| FCF | $52,103M | $43,585M | ~$9,308M |
| Buybacks | $30,125M | $26,248M | ~$10,000M [ASSUMPTION] |
| Net Cash (vs. LT debt) | +$49,002M | +$22,848M | ~+$14,000M [ASSUMPTION] |
| CapEx/Revenue | 22.6% | 34.7% | ~49% [ASSUMPTION] |
| OCF Consumed by CapEx | 40.8% | 60.2% | ~94% [ASSUMPTION] |

META is transitioning from a capital-light, cash-return compounder to a capital-intensive infrastructure company within 18 months. The credit rating holds. The equity story changes materially.

### At What Point Does Financial Flexibility Become a Concern?

Three scenarios would trigger genuine credit concern (currently low probability):

1. **Prolonged CapEx at $125B+ without revenue acceleration:** If 2027 CapEx guidance remains $125B+ and revenue growth decelerates to <15%, FCF/Debt falls below 10% and Moody's begins a review. Probability: [ASSUMPTION] 15-20%.

2. **Adverse legal outcomes exceeding $30B:** Unlikely but possible if youth mental health litigation produces landmark damages. Would require drawing down most of the cash cushion. Probability: [ASSUMPTION] 5-10%.

3. **Reality Labs pivot requiring accelerated investment:** Management has guided RL losses "at 2025 levels" in 2026. Any upward revision increases the drain. Probability: [ASSUMPTION] 20%.

### How Does Rising Debt Change the Equity Risk Profile?

Rising debt creates **operating leverage amplification**. In an advertising downturn:
- Fixed costs (data center leases, depreciation on $176B PP&E) are now much larger
- Interest expense is now ~$3.8B/year (up from ~$1.3B in 2023)
- FCF is now more volatile relative to operating income
- The margin of safety for equity holders narrows relative to the pre-leverage era

The flip side: if AI infrastructure generates incremental revenue streams (Meta AI subscriptions, third-party inference revenue, advertising efficiency gains), the leverage serves as a return amplifier on equity — the classic purpose of debt financing. The net impact on equity risk depends entirely on whether the $125B+ of AI infrastructure investment proves productive. **That is the central unresolved question of the investment thesis, and credit analysis cannot answer it.**

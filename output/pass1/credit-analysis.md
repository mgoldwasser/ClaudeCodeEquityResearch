## 1. Capital Structure Overview

Microsoft's capital structure has undergone a significant transformation over the past three years. While traditional long-term bond debt has declined steadily (from $47,237M in FY2023 to $40,262M as of December 2025), total debt has surged to ~$97,600M due to an explosion in finance lease obligations tied to AI/cloud data center infrastructure. This represents a 27.69% YoY increase in total debt -- the fastest expansion in Microsoft's modern history.

**Fixed vs. Floating Rate Mix:**
- Fixed-rate debt (senior unsecured notes): ~$43,262M (~44% of total) -- all investment-grade unsecured bonds carry fixed coupons
- Finance lease obligations (implicit fixed/variable): ~$46,200M (~47% of total) -- primarily fixed implicit rates
- Floating-rate exposure (commercial paper / short-term): ~$8,138M (~8% of total)
- **Net floating-rate exposure:** ~$8,138M (8.3% of total debt)

The floating-rate exposure is manageable. Each 100bps increase in short-term rates adds ~$81M to annual interest expense -- negligible relative to $162,681M EBITDA. [ASSUMPTION: Commercial paper estimated as residual between total debt and identifiable long-term + lease components.]

### Key Structural Observation

The most significant capital structure shift is the migration from traditional bonds to finance leases. Finance lease obligations (current + long-term) now represent ~47% of total debt, up from ~19% just three years ago. This reflects Microsoft's $100B+ annual CapEx commitment for AI data centers. [Source: CNBC, 2024-10-01; MacroTrends debt analysis; Microsoft Q2 FY2026 10-Q.]

**Uncommenced finance leases (off-balance-sheet):** $108,400M as of FY2025 10-K -- these represent committed but not yet started data center leases that will flow onto the balance sheet over the next 2-5 years. [Source: CNBC reporting on Microsoft 10-K footnote, October 2024 / July 2025.]

[Source: Web search results referencing Microsoft 10-K debt maturity schedule. Approximate figures. DATA GAP: exact maturity schedule by year not fully confirmed from 10-Q; figures from web aggregators.]

**Maturity Wall Assessment:**
- **Near-term maturity wall (2026-2027):** ~$13,250M (~30% of traditional LT bond debt) matures in the next ~18 months. This is manageable given ~$80B in available liquidity and the company's AAA credit rating, which ensures access to capital markets at any time.
- **Can the company refinance at reasonable rates?** Yes. Microsoft is one of only two U.S. corporates with AAA/Aaa ratings. Its bonds trade at near-sovereign spreads. Current refinancing rates would be approximately 4.5-5.0% for new long-dated issuance, compared to ~2.5-3.0% on maturing notes -- an increase of 150-200bps.
- **Refinancing cost delta:** If Microsoft refinances the full $13,250M maturing in 2026-2027 at 200bps higher rates, annual interest expense increases by ~$265M -- negligible relative to $162,681M EBITDA.

The maturity profile is well-laddered with no significant concentration risk. Over 80% of traditional bond debt matures after 2030, reflecting Microsoft's long-dated issuance strategy.

**Finance lease maturities** follow a different pattern -- they are tied to data center lease terms (typically 15-25 years) and create a steady stream of annual payments rather than a maturity wall. [ASSUMPTION: Finance lease payment schedule estimated as roughly linear over lease terms.]

**Commercial Paper Ratings:** A-1+ (S&P) / P-1 (Moody's) -- highest possible short-term ratings.

**Rating trajectory assessment:** **Stable with long-term watch.** Microsoft is one of only two U.S. corporates (with Johnson & Johnson) maintaining AAA/Aaa ratings from all three agencies. The rating agencies have thus far accepted the CapEx ramp as strategically necessary for maintaining competitive position in cloud/AI, supported by the company's exceptional cash generation.

**What would trigger a downgrade?**
Rating agencies have historically indicated that Microsoft's AAA would be at risk if:
- Net Debt/EBITDA sustained above 1.5x (currently 0.17x -- massive headroom)
- EBITDA/Interest sustained below 8.0x (currently ~71x)
- Sustained FCF deficit (FCF turned negative on a net basis after CapEx)
- A transformative debt-funded acquisition exceeding $100B+
- Sustained operating margin compression below 35% (currently ~47%)

At current trajectories, none of these triggers are close to being breached. However, if CapEx remains at $100B+ and revenue growth decelerates to single digits, the FCF deficit could become chronic by FY2028-2029, which would eventually draw rating agency scrutiny. [ASSUMPTION: Revenue growth deceleration to <10% by FY2028.]

**What would trigger an upgrade?** N/A -- AAA/Aaa is the highest possible rating.

**Key finding:** Microsoft can still fund its $100B+ CapEx program from operating cash flow WITHOUT issuing new debt -- but only by a razor-thin margin. **What breaks the model:** If revenue growth slows to <10% (from 17% currently) while CapEx remains elevated, OCF will no longer cover all cash needs. At that point, Microsoft would need to either:
1. Cut or pause share buybacks ($25B annual savings)
2. Issue new debt (which Morgan Stanley estimates could total $250-300B for hyperscalers collectively in 2026)

**Dividend sustainability:** The $3.32 annual dividend ($24.7B total) is well-covered at 2.9x by FCF even in the current CapEx-heavy environment. Dividend growth has been modest (~10% annually), and the 0.81% yield provides limited downside support but is not a constraint on the balance sheet.

The $60B buyback program has no expiration date, giving management flexibility to slow the pace without formally suspending it.

## Credit Summary

**Microsoft's balance sheet remains a formidable source of strength, but it is under more pressure than at any point in the past decade.** The company maintains AAA/Aaa ratings from all three agencies -- a distinction shared with only Johnson & Johnson -- and its 71x interest coverage and 0.17x net leverage ratios are so far above investment-grade thresholds as to make near-term credit risk essentially zero.

**The single biggest credit risk is the CapEx trajectory, not the debt itself.** Total debt has surged 28% YoY to ~$97,600M, driven almost entirely by finance lease obligations for AI data centers. Uncommenced finance leases add another $108,400M in off-balance-sheet commitments. When these leases commence over the next 2-5 years, on-balance-sheet debt could reach $150-180B. Meanwhile, operating cash flow (~$162B) is now almost fully consumed by CapEx ($100B+), shareholder returns ($50B), and debt service ($8.7B), leaving ~$3-6B in annual slack -- down from $30-50B just two years ago.

**The capital structure supports the equity thesis but introduces a new constraint it never had before.** Historically, Microsoft's balance sheet was a no-risk topic -- limitless financial flexibility with excess cash. Today, the company is operating at the edge of its cash generation capacity. If revenue growth slows to high single digits while CapEx remains elevated, Microsoft will face its first genuine capital allocation trade-off in a decade: CapEx vs. buybacks. The AAA rating itself is not at risk in any reasonable scenario over the next 2-3 years, but the *financial flexibility* that the AAA rating represents is being consumed by the AI infrastructure build-out. The balance sheet is not weak -- it is being weaponized for a generational bet on AI, and the credit question reduces to whether that bet pays off before the financial cushion erodes.

**Rating: Balance sheet strength 4/5** (down from 5/5 two years ago due to CapEx-driven leverage increase and thinning FCF margin).
